# Safety Policy Review: AgentBox Persistent Machine Plan

## 1. Verdict: **CLEAN WITH CHANGES**

The architecture is elegant and appropriately simple for one user. The Guardian/Operator split, operation registry as the center, and reuse-first posture are all correct. The safety model has the right instincts — approvals should be rare, Guardian should prefer autonomous repair, and the approval boundary is largely at completion/consolidation. But the plan gestures at a policy (`safe/confirm/forbidden`) without ever defining it, and that gap creates ambiguity about the single most important operational boundary: **when does Guardian fix things itself vs. when does it escalate?**

---

## 2. The One Most Important Design Pressure

**The safe/confirm/forbidden taxonomy is named but never populated.** Section 10 declares the categories exist. Guardian lists "safe actions" and "unsafe actions requiring confirmation" inline. But:

- **Forbidden actions are never enumerated anywhere.** If nothing is forbidden, say so explicitly. If credential rotation or mass worktree deletion is forbidden without a specific approval track, say so.
- **Safe vs. confirm boundaries are scattered.** Guardian has one list, the completion/approval section has another, and the Operator's safety boundaries are implicit (it "should still go through the same safety policy") but never enumerated for Operator-specific actions like `stop`, `restart`, `cleanup`, or `consolidate`.
- **The single-user simplification is underspecified at the safety layer.** The plan says "approvals should be rare and focused on completion, merge, cleanup, credentials, or accepting debt." That's the right spirit, but it never gets codified into a concrete policy the Guardian and Operator can actually enforce. Without an explicit list, the Guardian will either over-escalate (slowing Peter) or under-escalate (making decisions it shouldn't).

This is the pressure: the plan's safety instincts are correct, but they are gestural. A concrete, enumerated policy would cost ~15 lines of spec and prevent months of Guardian tuning guesswork.

---

## 3. Concrete Plan Change

Add an explicit **Safety Policy Table** to Section 10. Replace the current gestural text with something like:

```markdown
### Safety Policy Table

All AgentBox actions fall into one of three categories. Both Guardian and Operator enforce the same table.

**SAFE (no approval required, no notification):**
- Create operation records, worktrees, branches, tmux sessions
- Launch chains, plans, codex, claude, subagent, test, shell runs
- Read logs, state, registry, repo status
- Restart dead tmux sessions or resume stale chains (operational recovery)
- Push branches and open/update draft PRs (when operation policy permits)
- Classify and report operation state
- Run diagnostic subagents for unclear failures
- Credential health checks (read-only)
- Daily briefing compilation

**CONFIRM (requires Peter approval via Discord, one message round-trip):**
- Merge PR to main
- Delete worktrees or branches (destructive cleanup)
- Accept failing validation or known quality debt as complete
- Resolve ambiguous product decisions
- Rotate or expose credentials
- Stop or restart an operation Peter launched interactively
- Consolidate/land loose branches (cleanup-loose-branches survey)

**FORBIDDEN (never performed, Guardian or Operator must refuse):**
- Delete the operation registry or agentbox.db
- Modify another operation's worktree or branch outside a declared subagent relationship
- Push to main directly (merge via PR only)
- Execute credential rotation without an explicit approval record
- Run commands outside /workspace without a declared and approved path
```

This is small. It is exhaustive enough to remove ambiguity. It preserves the single-user bias: most actions are SAFE, CONFIRM is a short list of irreversible or quality-judgment actions, and FORBIDDEN is a safety rail. No ceremony beyond "one message round-trip" is added.

---

## 4. What I Would Explicitly Avoid Adding

**Never add intermediate-step approval gates.** The plan already says "The main approval event is completion, not every intermediate action." That's correct. Do not add:

- Per-step chain approval ("step 3/7 is done — continue?")
- Per-agent-launch approval ("Claude wants to run — allow?")
- Per-file-change approval
- Approval expiry or timeouts that re-prompt Peter on routine operations
- An approval queue UI or complex state machine for confirmations — one pending question per conversation scope, matched by the next message, is sufficient
- Role hierarchies, permission levels, or multi-user approval chains — this is a single-user system, and `safe/confirm/forbidden` should be a flat table, not a role-based access control model

The approval UX should remain: Peter sends a message, the system does the right thing 95% of the time, and the 5% that needs confirmation is a single Discord DM with concrete options. Anything that adds friction to the 95% path is overbuilding.
